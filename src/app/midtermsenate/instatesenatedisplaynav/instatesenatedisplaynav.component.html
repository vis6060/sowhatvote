Did you know?

Investments in Risky Cryptocurrency

One in five Americans have used or traded digital assets and that number goes up for historically in underserved communities. There is lot of interest to buy cryptocurrency assets such as Bitcoin and Ethereum due to the media attention and hype. The hype is from a few early success stories of people who earned extraordinary returns from their investment in crypto.

However, these are risky assets to invest in. They are risky due to their volatility, i.e., unexpected changes in markets and sentiments can result in huge swings in their price. They can quickly drop by a few thousands of dollars. Also, the prices are not regulated by the US government and are bought and sold on trading platforms which increases the chances of price changes due to technical glitches, human errors and hacking. Thus, these markets can lack the transparency and fairness that Americans expect from our financial system.

A recent (August 2022) bipartisan bill by Chairwoman Stabenow and Ranking Member Boozman gives the Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC) the tools and authorities it needs to protect consumers, prevent fraud and abuse, and create transparency and accountability in the digital commodity marketplace. Regulation by the CFTC could help protect consumers and empower them to make more informed trading decisions. This bill will be debated in the 2023 Congress session.

Please encourage your U.S. House of Representative and Senators to either adopt legislation or create some other way to protect our individual community members, who are going to feel the losses the most and may lose a lot of their invested assets.

Stablecoins?

The supposed value of all of digital assets in circulation recently passed three trillion dollars. A lot of people are investing in stablecoins which is a central component of the cryptocurrency ecosystem.

Stablecoins can speed up payments, especially cross-border transfers, reduce costs, and help combat money laundering and terrorist financing. Stablecoins are pegged to an actual asset. For example, one Stablecoin can always be made equal to one dollar. Stablecoins are being used by several American small businesses as traditional payment systems can be expensive and take several days to settle, transferring funds via stablecoins is low-cost and nearly instantaneous.

However, there are risks associated with it. As Senator Brown said Theres no guarantee youre going to get it back. They call it a currency, implying its the same as having dollars in the bank, and you can withdraw the money at any time. But many of these companies hide their terms and conditions in the fine print, allowing them to trap customers money.

The Senate Banking committee has held hearings and proposed frameworks. But, these are not implemented as yet. Senator Toomey proposed a creation of a new framework should protect the privacy, security, and confidentiality of individuals using stablecoins, allowing customers to opt out of sharing personal information with third parties.

Please encourage your U.S. House of Representative and Senators to either adopt legislation or create some other way to protect our individual community members, who are going to feel the losses the most and may lose a lot of their invested assets.

Did you know?

Rising home rents due to private equity ownership

Do you live in a city where home rents have been going up dramatically each year? One reason for the rent increase could be that a group of investors, referred to as Private equity institutional investors have bought these properties at low prices in cash or at foreclosure auctions after the 2008 housing crisis.

Neighborhoods with good schools in Atlanta, Phoenix, Tampa, and Charlotte became the centers of investment. By 2020, just seven cities contained more than half of the homes owned by institutional investors. The nearly 38,000 manufactured housing communities around the country have long been owned by small, local companies. The families who live there own their home, but rent the lot where their home is placed.

When big investors began buying up these communities after the crisis, many of them raised rents, added new fees, and changed their policies all without fixing up the communities the way they promised residents they would. This is the playbook of these big investors.

Today, in many cities and towns there are few affordable options. When private equity comes in and buys up homes in their town, many renters are left with no choice other than to accept the rising rents. They would have to rent at those higher prices, locking in tenants, with no other options, for years into the future.

Please encourage your U.S. House of Representative and Senators to either adopt legislation or create some other way to protect our communities from the burden of rising home rents.
