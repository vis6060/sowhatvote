Did you know?

Holding Big Corporations Accountable in Bankruptcy cases

In the last 20 years, more than 70% of public companies with at least $100 million in assets filed for bankruptcy in a district outside of the one closest to their headquarters. The legislation would prevent corporations from “shopping” for favorable bankruptcy judges and ensure that small businesses, employees, retirees, creditors, and other stakeholders can fully participate in cases that will have tremendous impacts on their lives.

Corporations seeking courts sympathetic to their interests often ‘forum shop,’ tilting the playing field away from small businesses.

Senators Warren and Cornyn introduced the Bankruptcy Venue Reform Act of 2021 in September 2021. However, it has not been signed into law. Such a reform would ensure that important bankruptcy cases would remain in their home states so that the distinct needs of each community are addressed and not overlooked or, worse, ignored by a remote court.

Senator Cornyn said: "I urge my colleagues to support this bipartisan, common-sense solution to close this loophole and help restore public trust in our bankruptcy system."

Please encourage your U.S. House of Representative and Senators to adopt meaningful legislation to hold the corporations accountable.

Did you know?

Bankruptcy Reforms to Help Individuals

More than 1.5 million people are filing for bankruptcy every year.

In 2020, the Consumer Bankruptcy Reform Act of 2020 was introduced in the Senate to simplify and modernize the consumer bankruptcy system to make it easier for individuals and families forced into bankruptcy to get back on their feet. Senator Nadler said- “the bankruptcy system has become complex, unfair, and even punitive for ordinary consumers. The Consumer Bankruptcy Reform Act ensures that the bankruptcy system works for the American people and not just big corporate creditors.”

The ongoing COVID-19 pandemic and economic crisis has left millions of Americans out of work, drowning in debt, and struggling under the weight of medical bills, student loans, mortgages, and car loans. Even before the pandemic hit, Americans were already hanging on by their fingernails. In the last 40 years, consumer credit skyrocketed as Americans, faced with stagnant wages, took on more debt to deal with the increasing costs of housing, education, medical care, and child care. For millions of households, a shock like a job loss or a family illness can send them tumbling over a financial cliff.

This reform act proposed to streamline the filing process and reduce the filing fees. During the bankruptcy proceedings, renters can avoid eviction, can discharge student loan debt, and can protect their home and cars, thus, they can quickly come back to their feet at the end of the bankruptcy proceeding. This act helps address the racial and gender disparities in the bankruptcy system. This act proposed to close the loopholes for the wealthy to exploit the bankruptcy system and end the predatory practices and hold the corporate wrongdoers accountable.

Please encourage your U.S. House of Representative and Senators to adopt meaningful legislation to fix the bankruptcy system, so community members can quickly get back on their feet after a bankruptcy episode.
